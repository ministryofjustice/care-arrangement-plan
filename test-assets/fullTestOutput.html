How making a child arrangements plan can help

You may find that collaborating with Bob on a child arrangements plan:

  • is cheaper and quicker than going to court (it usually takes around 10 months to get a court order, depending on where you live and whether it’s urgent)
  • leads to a better outcome for your children, which you and your ex-partner are in control of

Going to court can sometimes mean you are not the ones who make the final decisions about your child arrangements.

Tips to help you and your children

Your child arrangements plan should:

  • put the child’s welfare first
  • reflect the wishes, feelings and needs of the child
  • consider any harm the child has suffered or any risk of harm

If there is a court order in place

Do not continue if there’s a court order in place relating to you, the other parent, or the children.

For example, a:

  • child arrangements order
  • specific issue order
  • prohibited steps order
  • protective order such as a non-molestation order, occupation order or a domestic abuse protection order
  • any other order restricting contact between you and the other parent or the children

To change or enforce an existing child arrangements order, visit https://www.gov.uk/looking-after-children-divorce/change-or-enforce-an-order.

Getting help with domestic abuse

To find out more about signs and effects of child abuse and neglect, visit https://www.nspcc.org.uk/what-is-child-abuse.

If you’re unsure whether you’re a victim of domestic abuse, visit https://www.gov.uk/guidance/domestic-abuse-how-to-get-help#recognise-domestic-abuse.

This plan is not suitable for you if there has been:

  • any form of domestic abuse or violence, even if the abuse was not directed at the children
  • child abduction or attempted child abduction
  • child abuse or neglect
  • misuse of drugs, alcohol or other substances
  • any other safety or welfare concerns that place anyone at significant risk of harm

If you can't agree

If you are unable to reach agreement about your child arrangements, you can try mediation or another way of agreeing outside of court.

A mediator is a professional who will work with you to help you make decisions based on your child’s best interests. They listen to both of you and take a neutral approach.

More information and support is available at https://www.gov.uk/looking-after-children-divorce.
